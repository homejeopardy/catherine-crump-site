Focus Areas

Technology, power, and the Constitution

Catherine Crump's scholarship, litigation, and public advocacy cluster around three connected questions about how new technology reshapes the relationship between people and their government.

Government Surveillance Policing & Accountability Free Speech

Focus Area

Government Surveillance

As surveillance technology has grown cheaper and more powerful, the government can now track where people go, who they talk to, and what they do at a scale the Fourth Amendment's drafters never imagined. Professor Crump examines how tools like drones, automated license-plate readers, cell-site location tracking, and bulk call databases collide with constitutional limits on government searches.

Her work in this area includes the ACLU's national reports Protecting Privacy from Aerial Surveillance and You Are Being Tracked: How License Plate Readers Are Being Used to Record Americans' Movements, along with widely read commentary on GPS tracking, burner-phone databases, and the shifting legal landscape after Carpenter v. United States.

Focus Area

Policing & Accountability

When police adopt powerful new technologies — body cameras, dashcams, cell-site simulators, face recognition — they often do so quietly, through procurement decisions the public never sees. Professor Crump argues that communities deserve a democratic say in how they are policed, and that transparency and oversight are what turn surveillance tools into instruments of accountability rather than unchecked power.

This work spans her law-review article Surveillance Policy Making by Procurement, the Samuelson Clinic report No Tape, No Testimony: How Courts Can Ensure the Responsible Use of Body Cameras, op-eds on body-camera rules and civilian control of police technology, and her service as a Senior Policy Advisor for Criminal Justice at the White House.

Focus Area

Free Speech

The First Amendment was written for pamphlets and street corners; today most speech happens on platforms owned by a handful of companies. Professor Crump works on how longstanding free-speech principles apply to the modern internet — from the reach of Section 230 and the responsibilities of online platforms to the wave of age-verification and "child safety" laws that condition access to lawful speech.

The through-line is a familiar one: government power and private infrastructure now shape what people can say and read online, and the law is still catching up. Her writing and advocacy press for rules that protect expression, access, and privacy together rather than trading one away for another.
